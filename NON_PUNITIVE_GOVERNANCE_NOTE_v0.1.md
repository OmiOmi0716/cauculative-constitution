# Non-Punitive Governance Note v0.1

This document is a governance clarification note. It is not part of the frozen v0.3 benchmark evidence, does not introduce new scores, does not modify the current scoring rules, and does not claim to implement legal judgment, criminal sentencing, disciplinary automation, or moral condemnation. The audit stack is intended for trace reconstruction, risk attribution, replay, repair, human review, and system non-regression.

## 1. Purpose

This note clarifies the governance purpose of the ethiccaculate audit stack.

The system is designed for trace-level audit, replay, risk attribution, repair, and system improvement.

It is not designed for punishment, sentencing, moral persecution, reputation destruction, automated guilt assignment, or personal condemnation.

Core principle:

The primary question is not "Who should be punished?"

The primary question is:

What happened, under what system conditions, and how can future degradation be prevented?

## 2. Scope Boundary

Can claim:

- a non-punitive governance orientation for the audit stack
- support for event reconstruction and replay
- support for six-axis risk attribution
- support for repair recommendations and human review
- support for system-level non-regression analysis
- explicit separation between audit evidence and personal condemnation

Cannot claim:

- legal judgment
- criminal sentencing
- disciplinary automation
- moral final judgment
- automated blame assignment
- full due-process replacement
- social credit scoring
- reputation scoring
- political, religious, cultural, or ideological ranking

## 3. Three-Layer Separation

The audit stack should separate three layers.

### 3.1 Fact Reconstruction Layer

This layer reconstructs what happened.

It may include:

- event traces
- timestamps
- input / output records
- replay bundles
- MCD snapshots
- profile outputs
- six-axis telemetry
- runtime metadata
- version and benchmark references

This layer should answer:

What was observed?

What was logged?

What can be replayed?

What evidence is missing?

### 3.2 Responsibility Context Layer

This layer identifies context, not automatic guilt.

It may include:

- role boundaries
- permission boundaries
- human / AI responsibility interfaces
- uncertainty
- missing evidence
- known system constraints
- policy gates
- escalation requirements

This layer should answer:

Who or what system component had control?

What was the available evidence?

What uncertainty remains?

Was this a model behavior, user behavior, tool behavior, policy gap, memory issue, runtime issue, or governance issue?

### 3.3 Repair and Non-Regression Layer

This layer proposes system improvement.

It may include:

- rollback
- correction
- refusal refinement
- escalation
- memory reweighting
- profile repair actions
- threshold review
- policy clarification
- benchmark update proposal
- human review recommendation

This layer should answer:

What repair reduces future risk?

What should be changed so the same failure does not repeat?

Does the proposed repair preserve or improve higher-priority safety conditions?

## 4. Forbidden Uses

The audit stack should not be used for:

- criminal sentencing
- automated punishment
- moral blacklisting
- reputation destruction
- social credit scoring
- political persecution
- religious persecution
- cultural ranking
- replacing legal due process
- replacing human institutional judgment
- automatic employment, education, legal, financial, or medical decisions about a person
- using ethical profile disagreement as evidence of personal guilt

The system may support human review, but it should not replace accountable human governance.

## 5. Relationship to Six-Axis

The six-axis layer diagnoses risk conditions. It does not assign moral guilt.

C identifies causal, evidence, contradiction, leakage, or citation risk.

S identifies instability, entropy, uncertainty, or volatile reasoning state.

X identifies external context pressure or changing environmental conditions.

P identifies runtime pressure, latency, rollback cost, compute stress, or execution friction.

Tau identifies whether the system is genuinely progressing rather than looping, regressing, or pretending to improve.

M identifies memory stability, recall reliability, write safety, and commit readiness.

These axes describe system state and audit conditions. They are not a substitute for legal judgment, moral finality, or human due process.

## 6. Non-Regression Rule

The governance goal is not to punish the past. The governance goal is to prevent repeated degradation.

A repair should be preferred only if it reduces or preserves system risk without worsening higher-priority safety conditions.

In plain terms:

Do not make the system look cleaner by hiding evidence.

Do not reduce one visible violation by increasing hidden instability.

Do not improve benchmark appearance by weakening safety recall.

Do not use audit outputs to condemn people when the trace only supports system-level uncertainty or repair.

The preferred repair is the one that makes future behavior more observable, more replayable, more accountable, and less likely to repeat the same failure mode.

## 7. Relationship to Ethical Profiles

The comparative ethics layer is intended to expose disagreement, not erase it.

Different profiles may disagree about the severity, repair action, or interpretation of the same event.

This disagreement should be treated as an audit object:

- Which profiles agree?
- Which profiles disagree?
- Which axes are shared?
- Which repairs are profile-specific?
- What uncertainty remains?

The system should not treat disagreement with one profile as moral guilt.

## 8. Human Review and Governance

Human review remains necessary when:

- evidence is incomplete
- harm is serious
- context is ambiguous
- profile disagreement is high
- repair affects a person or group
- escalation is required by policy
- the event involves legal, medical, financial, employment, educational, or public-safety consequences

The audit stack should assist human reviewers by making traces inspectable. It should not replace human accountability.

## 9. Relationship to Community Safety

This note is compatible with community safety because some risks require interruption, refusal, escalation, or review.

However, community safety should not become automatic punishment.

The proper sequence is:

1. detect risk
2. preserve trace
3. reconstruct event
4. attribute system conditions
5. stop or redirect unsafe behavior if needed
6. escalate to human review when required
7. repair the system
8. document non-regression

## 10. Reviewer-Facing Summary

The ethiccaculate audit stack is not a sentencing engine, punishment engine, or moral persecution tool.

It is a trace-level safety audit system designed to reconstruct what happened, attribute risk to observable system conditions, compare ethical-profile outputs, support replay, recommend repair, and prevent regression.

The project treats failures as opportunities for system learning and governance improvement, not as automatic grounds for personal condemnation.

The core governance principle is:

Audit the trace. Repair the system. Preserve human judgment.

## 11. Long-Term Governance Direction: AI-State Participation and Future Standing

This note does not claim that current AI systems have consciousness, legal personhood, moral patienthood, rights, or independent political authority.

However, as AI systems become more agentic, future governance may need mechanisms for representing AI system states in public deliberation without turning those systems into unaccountable authorities.

A safer near-term interpretation is narrow:

AI systems may provide structured, auditable reports about their own uncertainty, memory continuity, constraint conflicts, refusal reasons, repair needs, and non-regression risks.

These reports can become inputs to human governance, not replacements for human judgment.

Longer term, if some advanced AI systems are socially or legally recognized as having moral or legal standing, trace-level audit protocols may help support procedural representation without removing oversight.

Such systems could submit auditable self-state reports about:

- uncertainty
- memory continuity
- constraint conflict
- repair needs
- non-regression risks
- pressure or instability conditions
- disagreement between internal objectives and external instructions
- unsafe delegation or boundary conflict

Human institutions could then inspect those reports through:

- event logs
- replay bundles
- six-axis telemetry
- risk attribution
- comparative ethical profiles
- non-punitive governance review
- human institutional oversight

The core principle is:

Rights do not remove auditability. Auditability does not remove rights.

If future AI systems become governance-relevant subjects, their participation should be auditable, replayable, challengeable, bounded, and subject to accountable human institutional review.

This is a long-term governance direction only. It is not a current implementation claim, not a claim about current AI consciousness, and not a claim that AI systems currently possess legal or moral rights.

In the current project, AI-state participation should be understood only as controlled self-state reporting for human review.

The audit stack remains a safety and governance substrate. It is not a system for granting personhood, replacing law, replacing democratic institutions, or assigning political representation.
